What Is Small Claims Court?

Small claims court is a division of the California Superior Court designed for everyday disputes involving money. It's faster, cheaper, and less formal than regular civil court — and critically, lawyers cannot represent either party at the hearing in most California small claims cases. You argue your own case directly to the judge.

In California, individuals can claim up to $12,500. Businesses are limited to $6,250. If your dispute is within these limits, small claims is almost always the right venue.

Before You File — Send a Demand Letter

California doesn't legally require a demand letter before filing, but judges expect to see that you made a reasonable attempt to resolve the dispute before bringing it to court. A demand letter serves two purposes: it often results in payment before you need to file, and if ignored, it becomes strong evidence in your favor.

What a proper demand letter includes:

  • A clear statement of what is owed and why
  • A specific deadline for payment (10–14 days is standard)
  • A statement that you will file in small claims court if not paid
  • Your contact information and signature
  • Reference to any contract, agreement, or verbal promise made
Pro Tip

Send your demand letter via certified mail with return receipt. Keep the green card — it proves they received it and becomes exhibit A in your case if they ignore it.

Step-by-Step: How to File in Kern County

1

Fill out Form SC-100

Download the Plaintiff's Claim and Order to Go to Small Claims Court (SC-100) from the California Courts website or pick one up at the Kern County courthouse. Fill out your name, the defendant's name and address, the amount you're claiming, and a brief description of what happened.

2

File at the Kern County Courthouse

Bring your completed SC-100 and payment for the filing fee to the small claims window at 1415 Truxtun Avenue. The clerk will stamp your forms, assign a case number, and give you a hearing date — usually 30–70 days out.

3

Serve the Defendant

The defendant must be officially notified of your lawsuit before the hearing. California has specific service requirements — you cannot serve the papers yourself. See the service section below for your options.

4

File Your Proof of Service

After the defendant is served, the person who served them completes a Proof of Service (SC-104) form. This must be filed with the court at least 5 days before your hearing for personal service, or 15 days for mail service.

5

Appear at Your Hearing

Show up on time with all your evidence organized. Present your case clearly and calmly. The judge will ask questions and typically issues a decision the same day.

If you win, you can ask the judge to include your filing fee in the judgment — meaning the defendant reimburses you.

Fee Waiver Available

If you cannot afford the filing fee, you may qualify for a fee waiver. Ask the clerk for Form FW-001 (Request to Waive Court Fees) when you file.

Serving the Defendant in California

Service means officially notifying the defendant that they are being sued. You have several options:

  • Certified mail service — The court clerk can serve by certified mail for a small fee. This is the easiest option for most plaintiffs.
  • Personal service by a process server — A registered process server delivers papers in person. More reliable, costs $50–$100.
  • Kern County Sheriff — The sheriff's civil division can serve papers for a fee. Call (661) 868-4170.
  • Substituted service — If the defendant can't be found, you can leave papers with a competent adult at their home or workplace.

What Happens at the Hearing

Kern County small claims hearings typically last 10–20 minutes. The judge hears both sides, may ask clarifying questions, and usually renders a decision the same day.

Bring these to your hearing:

  • Your original claim form and all copies
  • Your demand letter and proof it was sent (certified mail receipt)
  • All contracts, written agreements, or estimates
  • Text messages or emails (printed and organized by date)
  • Photos, videos, or physical evidence
  • Receipts and invoices
  • Witness statements if applicable
Judges in small claims courts hear dozens of cases in a morning. The person with organized, clear evidence wins more often than the person with more righteous anger. Keep it factual and chronological.

After the Judgment — Collecting Your Money

Winning the judgment and collecting the money are two different things. If the defendant doesn't pay voluntarily, you have legal tools to enforce the judgment:

  • Wage garnishment — Up to 25% of disposable earnings per pay period
  • Bank levy — Freeze and collect from the defendant's bank account
  • Property lien — Place a lien on real property they own
  • Till tap / keeper levy — For businesses, seize cash from registers

Judgments in California are valid for 10 years and can be renewed. The defendant's credit is also affected, which often motivates payment.
