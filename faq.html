California Small Claims Law

As of 2026, the limits are:

  • Individuals: up to $12,500 per claim
  • Businesses, corporations, or public entities: up to $6,250
  • You may file up to 2 claims per calendar year that exceed $2,500

Verify current limits at kern.courts.ca.gov as these can change.

No — and in most cases, lawyers cannot represent you at the hearing anyway. California Code of Civil Procedure Section 116.530 generally prohibits attorneys from representing parties at small claims hearings. Both sides argue their own cases directly to the judge.

This is actually a feature, not a bug — it levels the playing field and keeps costs low for everyone.

The time limit to file depends on your case type:

  • Written contracts: 4 years from the breach
  • Oral contracts: 2 years from the breach
  • Property damage: 3 years
  • Personal injury: 2 years

If you're close to the deadline, file sooner rather than later. Missing the statute of limitations means you lose your right to sue regardless of how strong your case is.

A judgment in your favor is a legally enforceable court order. If the defendant doesn't pay voluntarily, you have enforcement tools including:

  • Wage garnishment — up to 25% of their disposable earnings
  • Bank account levy — freeze and collect from their accounts
  • Property lien — clouds their title if they own real estate
  • Vehicle levy — seize and sell a registered vehicle

California judgments are valid for 10 years and renewable. The judgment also shows on their credit report, which motivates most people to pay.

From filing to hearing is typically 30–70 days in Kern County, depending on the court's calendar. After the hearing, if you win and the defendant still doesn't pay, enforcement can take additional weeks or months depending on their assets and cooperation.

Total timeline from "I want to file" to "I have my money" is typically 3–6 months for cooperative defendants, longer if enforcement is needed.
